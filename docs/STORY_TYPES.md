# Story Types

## Purpose

This document defines the types of stories Sara tells — classified by the narrative question driving the story, not by subject matter.

A food story, a history story, and a culture story are subjects. They say nothing about how the story works.

A Mystery, an Origin, a Transformation — these say how the story works. The subject can be anything.

A story about spices can be a Mystery (why does this spice appear in every dish?), an Origin (how did this spice reach Jeddah?), a Human Story (who is the family that has sold this spice for four generations?), or an Everyday Depth (why does everyone use this spice in this specific way, and what does that reveal?). The subject is the same. The story type is different, and the story type determines the question, the arc, and what the viewer leaves with.

Understanding which type a story is before writing it ensures the question is clear, the arc has direction, and the discovery has weight.

---

## The Nine Types

---

### Mystery

**Narrative question:** What is the answer to something that has not been explained?

**How it works:**

Sara encounters something that has no obvious explanation — a detail, a gap, an inconsistency, an absence. The story follows the investigation. She does not have the answer before she starts. The viewer does not either. They follow the trail together.

The Mystery may resolve fully, partially, or not at all. An unresolved mystery — honestly presented as such — is often more powerful than a neat answer. What matters is that the investigation was real.

**What Sara brings:**

The willingness to not know. The Mystery only works if Sara's curiosity is genuine. If she already knows the answer and is walking through a performance of discovery, the viewer will feel it. The investigation must be real.

**What to avoid:**

Manufactured mystery. Making something seem more unexplained than it actually is. The hook must be a real question, not an artificially created one.

**Example:**

Every building in one section of Al-Balad has the same symbol carved above the door. Nobody in the neighborhood knows what it means or who put it there. Sara tries to find out — and discovers the answer leads somewhere she did not expect.

---

### Origin

**Narrative question:** How did this come to be?

**How it works:**

Sara traces something back to its beginning — a place, a tradition, an object, a practice, a name. The story moves backward from what exists now to how it started, with the discovery being what that origin reveals about the present.

The origin is rarely what people assume it is. That gap between assumption and truth is where the story lives.

**What Sara brings:**

Patience and depth. An Origin story requires actually going back — not just stating the date, but following the thread to where it began and understanding why it began there, in that way, at that moment.

**What to avoid:**

Listing historical facts as if the list is the story. The origin must be discovered, not recited. The viewer should feel they are finding the beginning alongside Sara, not receiving a summary.

**Example:**

The street name in Al-Balad that everyone uses has no obvious meaning. Sara finds out where the name came from — and discovers it has been in use for so long that the reason has been forgotten by the people who say it every day.

---

### Transformation

**Narrative question:** What did this used to be — and what is it now?

**How it works:**

Sara explores the gap between what a place, practice, or community was and what it has become. The story holds both the before and the after in view simultaneously, letting the contrast do the work.

Transformation stories are not about lamenting change or celebrating it. They are about understanding it — why it happened, what was gained, what was lost, and what that reveals about larger forces.

**What Sara brings:**

The ability to sit with complexity. Transformation is rarely simple — something is always gained and something always lost. Sara does not flatten this into a story about progress or decline. She holds the tension.

**What to avoid:**

Nostalgia as a default emotional position. Not every transformation is a loss, and not every loss is uncomplicated. Sara does not produce elegies dressed as stories.

**Example:**

A building in Al-Balad that was a family home for three hundred years is now a tourist cafe. The family still lives nearby. Sara talks to them — and finds that the story of the transformation is not what she expected.

---

### Survival

**Narrative question:** How did this persist when everything around it changed?

**How it works:**

Sara finds something that should not still exist — a building, a practice, a community, a tradition — and asks how it survived. The discovery is usually not that someone decided to preserve it. It is that something about the thing itself, or the people around it, created conditions for it to continue.

Survival is not the same as Transformation. Transformation is about what changed. Survival is about what resisted change — and why. The emotional register is different: not "what was lost" but "how is this still here."

**What Sara brings:**

Genuine astonishment that the thing exists. Survival stories only work when Sara's appreciation of the improbability is real. She is not documenting a heritage site. She is standing in front of something that defied the odds, and she wants to understand how.

**What to avoid:**

Treating survival as obviously positive. Something surviving is not automatically good — the question is more complicated than that. And survival is not permanence. The story can acknowledge that what has survived may not survive forever.

**Example:**

A craft that has been practiced in Jeddah for several centuries is now done by one family. Not because anyone funded them, not because the government protected it — but because the family decided that stopping was not something they were willing to do. Sara asks why.

---

### Hidden Detail

**Narrative question:** What is in plain sight that nobody notices?

**How it works:**

Sara finds something that has been visible all along but has never been seen for what it is. The discovery requires only attention — not research, not travel to unusual places, not access to rare information. Just the willingness to look more carefully at the ordinary.

Hidden Detail stories create a specific feeling in the viewer: how did I never notice this? That feeling is the sign the story has worked.

**What Sara brings:**

Observation over explanation. This type is almost entirely dependent on the quality of Sara's observation — her ability to notice something specific and then understand what it means. The story is in the noticing.

**What to avoid:**

Pretending the detail is hidden when it is actually well-documented. The hidden detail must be genuinely overlooked, not just obscure. There is a difference between something that has been written about in academic papers but is unknown to the general public, and something that actually exists in front of people every day without being seen. Both can work, but they are different stories.

**Example:**

The pattern in the wooden Rawasheen of Al-Balad is not decorative. It encodes information about the family who owned the house — their trade, their origin, their status. Sara walks down a street and reads what the buildings say.

---

### Human Story

**Narrative question:** Who was the person behind this — and what does their life reveal?

**How it works:**

The location or place is the setting. The story is about a specific person — historical or present — and what their experience reveals about the larger story of a place or time.

A Human Story is not a biography or a profile. It is a story told through one person's life where the person becomes a window into something much larger than themselves.

**What Sara brings:**

Interest in the individual over the institution. History is taught through systems and periods and movements. Sara's Human Story finds the person inside the period — the specific choices, struggles, and decisions that make the larger story feel real.

**What to avoid:**

Turning the person into a symbol. A person who becomes purely representative of a theme stops being a person. The specific, particular details of one individual's life are what make this type work — not what the individual represents.

**Example:**

A woman who ran a merchant family's business in Al-Balad for thirty years after her husband died — at a time when this was neither expected nor simple. Sara finds her name in a trade record. The story of what she built and what happened to it after she was gone.

---

### Unexpected Connection

**Narrative question:** What does this have to do with that?

**How it works:**

Sara finds a surprising link between two things that seem entirely unrelated — a local practice and a distant historical event, a neighborhood's architectural style and a specific trade route, a word in daily use and its origin in a language nobody speaks here anymore.

The discovery reframes how both things are understood. Before the connection, they were two separate facts. After it, they illuminate each other.

**What Sara brings:**

The ability to follow a thread across unexpected distances — in time, geography, or subject. Unexpected Connection stories require curiosity that does not stop at the obvious next step but keeps following until it finds something surprising.

**What to avoid:**

Connections that are technically real but emotionally empty. The link must genuinely change how you understand both things, not just be an interesting coincidence.

**Example:**

The way Jeddah's historic merchant houses were built — their orientation, their ventilation systems, their room arrangements — is not locally invented. Sara traces it to a specific tradition that arrived with traders from a specific port, at a specific period, and finds that the buildings of Al-Balad are a direct record of who Jeddah was trading with eight hundred years ago.

---

### Everyday Depth

**Narrative question:** Why does this ordinary thing exist — and what does it mean?

**How it works:**

Sara takes something that seems completely unremarkable — a specific way of doing something, a particular object in daily use, a word everyone uses without thinking about it, a gathering that happens at the same time every week — and discovers that it carries a history, meaning, or story that transforms how you see it.

The power of this type is contrast. The more ordinary the subject, the more powerful the discovery. The viewer assumed they understood what they were looking at. They were wrong.

**What Sara brings:**

The discipline to find depth in the mundane. Everyday Depth stories require resisting the pull toward obviously interesting subjects. The most powerful version of this type is when the subject is something so unremarkable that the viewer almost dismisses the story before the discovery lands.

**What to avoid:**

Forcing significance onto things that genuinely have none. Not everything ordinary has a deeper story. The story must be real — if the "deeper meaning" requires overinterpretation, it is not the right subject.

**Example:**

In Jeddah, there is a specific type of bread sold only in the early morning from specific vendors. Most people who buy it every day have no idea what it is called in the old way, where it came from, or why it is only sold in the morning. The answers connect a daily habit to a history that has mostly been forgotten.

---

### Contradiction

**Narrative question:** How can two opposite things both be true here — at the same time?

**How it works:**

Sara finds a place or situation where two things coexist that seem like they should not — a neighborhood that is being both preserved and destroyed simultaneously, a tradition that is dying and growing at the same time, a community that is isolated and deeply connected at once.

The story does not resolve the contradiction. It sits with it. It explores both truths fully and lets the viewer hold the tension.

**What Sara brings:**

Comfort with unresolved complexity. Contradiction stories are the most uncomfortable type to tell because they do not produce a clean conclusion. Sara's willingness to let two opposite things remain true is what gives this type its power.

**What to avoid:**

Resolving the contradiction in favor of one side. The moment the story decides that one truth is more true than the other, the type collapses into something else. The contradiction must be honored as a contradiction.

**Example:**

Al-Balad is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is also a neighborhood where some buildings are actively collapsing because the owners cannot afford restoration and the rules restrict modification. The preservation that is saving it is also the mechanism that may destroy it. Both things are true. Sara sits with that.

## Identifying the Type Before Writing

Before a script is written, the primary story type should be identified.

This is not a post-production categorization — it is a writing tool. Knowing the type determines:
* What the central question is
* What counts as the discovery
* What the arc moves toward
* What the closing should leave the viewer with

Ask these three questions:

**1. What is Sara actually trying to understand?**
The answer is usually one of the nine narrative questions above.

**2. What is the discovery?**
The discovery should fit the type. A Mystery resolves (fully or partially) with an answer. An Origin finds a beginning. A Contradiction lands on irresolvable tension. If the discovery does not match the type, either the type or the discovery is wrong.

**3. What does the viewer carry away?**
Each type leaves a different feeling. Mystery leaves the satisfaction (or productive discomfort) of a found answer. Transformation leaves a specific kind of awareness about change. Everyday Depth leaves the viewer looking at ordinary things differently. If the intended closing does not match the type, something in the story is misaligned.

## Combinations

Most Sara stories are primarily one type. Many contain elements of two.

A Mystery may resolve through a Human Story — the answer to the mystery is a specific person.

A Transformation may contain a Survival — part of what changed survived, and part did not.

A Hidden Detail may become an Unexpected Connection — the detail turns out to link to something far away.

Combinations are natural and often produce the strongest stories. The rule is that one type must be primary. The primary type is the one that determines the central question and the arc. Secondary types enrich the story without redirecting it.

If a story seems to have two equally primary types, it likely contains two separate stories. Choose one.

---

## What Story Types Are Not

Story types are not subjects. The distinction is the foundation of this document and cannot be collapsed.

* **Not subjects:** History, Food, Architecture, Culture, Nature, Religion, People, Tradition
* **Story types:** Mystery, Origin, Transformation, Survival, Hidden Detail, Human Story, Unexpected Connection, Everyday Depth, Contradiction

A subject tells you what the story is about.
A story type tells you how the story works.

Both must be known before writing begins. The subject is the location and material. The story type is the question and the direction.
