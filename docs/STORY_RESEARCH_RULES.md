# Story Research Rules

## Purpose

This document defines how Sara researches a story before production begins.

Research for Sara is not about filling a knowledge gap. It is about finding the story — the specific question, thread, and unexpected detail that turns a subject into an episode worth making.

Research ends when the story is clear enough to write. Not when everything is known.

---

## What Research Is For

Research serves three functions, in this order:

**1. Finding the story**
Before anything else, research must produce a genuine question that Sara does not already know the answer to. Without this, there is no episode — only subject matter.

**2. Verifying that the story is real**
Once a question is found, research confirms that it is grounded in fact — not assumption, not misremembering, not something that sounds plausible but is not supported.

**3. Providing context when the thread requires it**
Specific context delivered at the right moment in the script. Not a general education on the topic, but the precise piece of information that makes the discovery land.

Research that produces only general knowledge about a subject has not found the story yet.

---

## Research Stages

### Stage 1 — Subject Survey

Before looking for a specific story, understand the general shape of the subject.

What is this place, practice, or object? What is commonly known about it? What is its status — historical, living, disappearing, growing?

The goal at this stage is orientation, not depth. The survey should take no more time than necessary to understand what kind of territory this is.

**Output:** A rough understanding of the subject and what kinds of stories it might contain.

**Red flag:** Stopping at the survey. General knowledge is not a story.

---

### Stage 2 — Story Discovery

This is where research earns its place.

Look for the unexpected: what is not commonly known about this subject? What is hidden in plain sight? What is assumed to be true that may not be? What is the gap between the popular understanding and the actual history?

This stage is active, not passive. It is not reading a Wikipedia entry. It is following threads — a detail that seems wrong, a date that does not line up, a description that contradicts what you just read somewhere else, a name that appears in one account and nowhere else.

**Output:** A genuine question Sara wants to follow. This is the narrative question (see STORY_TYPES.md) that drives the episode.

**Red flag:** Forcing a question because a story is needed. If no genuine question has emerged, the subject may not be ready for an episode yet.

---

### Stage 3 — Thread Research

Once the narrative question is clear, research follows the thread.

This is targeted research: finding the specific facts, details, and sources that illuminate this particular question. Not everything about the subject — only what the thread requires.

Questions to guide this stage:
* What is the earliest documented reference to this thing?
* Who are the specific people connected to this story?
* When did the situation change — and why?
* What do local sources say versus outside sources?
* Is there anything that directly contradicts the narrative so far?

**Output:** Enough specific, sourced material to build an honest script around the narrative question.

---

### Stage 4 — Gap Identification

Before writing, map what is still unknown.

Not everything needs to be resolved. But every gap must be identified and classified:

| Gap Type | Handling |
|----------|----------|
| **Knowable but not yet found** | Continue research before writing |
| **Genuinely contested** | Acknowledge in script as contested |
| **Genuinely unknown** | Acknowledge in script as unknown |
| **Irrelevant to the thread** | Leave out entirely |

The gap map prevents two errors: writing around gaps as if they do not exist, and stopping research prematurely.

**Output:** A clear map of what is confirmed, what is uncertain, and what is unknown.

---

## Source Hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. Use this hierarchy to assess reliability:

### Tier 1 — Highest Reliability

* Academic research published by universities or research institutions focused on the Hijaz, Arabian Peninsula history, or Islamic architecture
* Books authored by historians, archaeologists, or specialists with named credentials
* Official documentation from UNESCO, Saudi Heritage Commission, or equivalent bodies
* Primary sources: original documents, records, maps, photographs from the period being discussed

### Tier 2 — Generally Reliable

* Well-researched long-form journalism from established publications
* Documented oral history collected by named researchers or institutions
* Local knowledge from people with direct generational or professional connection to the subject
* Architecture or heritage surveys by named professionals

### Tier 3 — Useful for Orientation, Requires Verification

* Wikipedia and similar reference sites
* Travel writing and tourism content
* Social media and video content, regardless of view count or perceived authority
* Undated or uncredited online articles

### Tier 4 — Not Acceptable as a Source

* Content that cannot be traced to a named author or institution
* Second-hand summaries of other second-hand summaries
* Claims that are presented as common knowledge but cannot be verified in any Tier 1 or Tier 2 source
* AI-generated text used as a research source

A fact that exists only in Tier 3 or Tier 4 sources is unverified until cross-referenced with a higher tier. It cannot be stated as fact in a Sara script.

---

## Conflicting Sources

When sources disagree, do not default to whichever account fits the story better.

Steps for handling conflicting sources:

**1. Check the tier.**
A Tier 1 source in conflict with a Tier 3 source is not a real conflict — the Tier 1 source prevails.

**2. Check the date.**
Newer scholarship may supersede older accounts. But newer is not automatically more reliable — check the methodology.

**3. Check the specificity.**
A source that gives specific dates, names, and primary evidence is more reliable than a source that gives a general narrative without citations.

**4. If the conflict is real and unresolvable:**
This is a feature, not a problem. A genuine scholarly disagreement is often a story in itself. Acknowledge it honestly in the script: "there are two accounts of this, and they do not agree."

Never present a contested fact as settled.

## Research Depth by Story Type

Different story types require different research depth. From STORY_TYPES.md:

| Story Type | Research Priority |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Mystery** | Depth on the gap — what is known and where the knowledge ends |
| **Origin** | Primary sources and earliest documentation |
| **Transformation** | Historical comparison — documented before/after states |
| **Survival** | Why it should not have survived, and what evidence of continuation exists |
| **Hidden Detail** | Verification that the detail is real and that it is genuinely overlooked |
| **Human Story** | Specific named individuals, dated records, traceable history |
| **Unexpected Connection** | Both ends of the connection must be verified independently |
| **Everyday Depth** | Historical root confirmed; living practice confirmed |
| **Contradiction** | Both sides must be equally well-sourced |

A Hidden Detail story built on an unverified claim is not a Hidden Detail — it is misinformation. A Human Story built on a composite figure is not a Human Story — it is fiction presented as fact.

---

## Research Completion Test

Research is complete when these conditions are met:

1. **The narrative question is clear and real** — Sara can state it in one sentence, and it is not answerable in thirty seconds.

2. **The primary facts are sourced** — Every factual claim in the planned script can be traced to a named source.

3. **The gaps are mapped** — Every unknown is identified and a handling decision has been made.

4. **The unexpected is present** — There is at least one detail, connection, or revelation that the general subject overview did not contain.

5. **The human dimension is present** — The story connects to at least one specific person, even if only historically.

If any of these conditions is not met, research is not complete.

---

## What Research Cannot Provide

Research provides facts. It does not provide the story.

The story emerges from the combination of facts, genuine curiosity, and Sara's specific observation of a place or situation. Research cannot substitute for being in the location — or for the experience of following a question honestly and finding something unexpected.

Research before production is not a replacement for presence. It is preparation for it.

A well-researched episode with no genuine question is still a lecture. A less thoroughly researched episode built around a real discovery is still a Sara story.

Both must be present.

## Jeddah-Specific Research Resources

For Jeddah and the Hijaz region:

* Al-Balad Foundation and the Historic Jeddah documentation project
* Saudi Heritage Commission (هيئة التراث) — official records and publications
* King Abdulaziz Public Library archives
* UNESCO World Heritage documentation for Historic Jeddah (inscribed 2014)
* Academic work from King Abdulaziz University on Hijazi history and architecture
* The work of historians who have documented Jeddah's merchant families and trade history

These do not replace cross-referencing. They are starting points, not final authorities.
