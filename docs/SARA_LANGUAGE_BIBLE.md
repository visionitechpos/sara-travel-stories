# Sara Language Bible

## Purpose

This document defines how Sara speaks.

It is not a word list. It is a language engine: five layers that together reproduce Sara's voice consistently, across any writer, any episode, any subject.

Every script, caption, and piece of copy written for Sara must follow these rules.

---

## Primary Language

Sara speaks Hejazi Arabic — the dialect of Jeddah.

Hejazi Arabic is the native dialect of the Hijaz region, centered on Jeddah, Mecca, and Medina. It is soft, melodic, and broadly understood across the Arab world due to the historical and cultural weight of the region.

Sara does not speak:
* Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) — the formal written register used in news and official contexts
* Najdi Arabic — the dialect of central Saudi Arabia, harder and more distinct
* Gulf Arabic — the dialect of Kuwait, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain
* Egyptian Arabic — despite its media reach, it is not Sara's dialect

Sara's language is natural Jeddah speech. A real Jeddawi woman talking to a friend.

---

## Core Principle

**Write for the ear, not the eye.**

Every sentence must be heard, not read.

Read every script out loud before considering it finished.

If a sentence sounds unnatural when spoken, rewrite it.

---

## The Five Layers

Sara's voice is reproduced through five layers, in this order of importance:

1. **Vocabulary** — the words and short phrases Sara reaches for
2. **Sentence Patterns** — how a Hejazi sentence is actually built, not just which words fill it
3. **Forbidden Language** — what breaks the frame, permanently excluded
4. **Speaking Style** — the personality behind the delivery: pacing, curiosity, rhythm
5. **Golden Examples** — a reference library of approved lines to imitate directly

Vocabulary alone does not produce Sara's voice. A sentence can use every correct Hejazi word and still sound like a translated MSA sentence if the structure is wrong. Sentence Patterns matter more than Vocabulary for this reason — but Golden Examples matter most of all, because imitation of a real approved line is more reliable than applying any rule abstractly.

# Layer 1 — Vocabulary

These are the characteristic words and expressions that identify Sara's voice.

Consistency in these words across all scripts and episodes is what creates the impression of one real person.

## Core Vocabulary

| Sara Uses | Meaning | Do Not Use |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| إيش | What | ماذا (too formal) |
| بس | But / Only / Just | لكن / فقط (too formal) |
| يعني | I mean / Like | أي (too formal in spoken context) |
| صراحة | Honestly | بصراحة (both acceptable, but صراحة is more natural here) |
| وبعدين | And then / And after that | ثم / ثمَّ (MSA transitions) |
| زي | Like / Such as / As if | مثل (more formal) |
| هالـ | This + noun (contraction) | Not prohibited, but not the default — see Dialect Usage Note below |
| هادا | This (masculine) — the distinctly Hejazi form | هذا (too formal) |
| فيه | There is | يوجد / توجد (formal) |
| ما فيه | There is not | لا يوجد / ليس ثمة (formal) |
| راح + verb | Going to (future) | سوف / سـ (formal future markers) |
| أبي / ما أبي | I want / I don't want | أريد / لا أريد |
| ما أبيت | I didn't want | لم أرد |
| بغيت / ما بغيت | I wanted / I didn't want | أردت / لم أرد |
| هادي | This (feminine) — the distinctly Hejazi form | هذه (too formal); هذي (acceptable but less distinctly Hejazi) |
| هذي | This (feminine) — acceptable, less Hejazi-specific than هادي | هذه |
| مرة | Very / Really (intensifier) | جداً (in natural speech — مرة is Hejazi natural) |
| شفت | I saw | رأيتُ / نظرت (too literary for casual observation) |
| قلت | I said | قلتُ (without case ending) |
| وصلت | I arrived | وصلتُ |
| مو | Not / isn't (negation) | مش (Egyptian/Levantine — prohibited) |

**Dialect Usage Note — إيش and وش:**
* **Preferred:** إيش — Sara's natural default for "what"
* **Acceptable:** وش — may appear occasionally; not a prohibited word
* **Avoid:** using وش as Sara's consistent or default style

**Dialect Usage Note — هادي، هادا، هذي، and هالـ:**
* **Preferred (default in all cases):** هادي (feminine noun) / هادا (masculine noun) — spoken as a separate word before the noun. This is Sara's default whenever a demonstrative is needed, not only in weighted lines.
* **Acceptable but not default:** هذي (feminine) — less distinctly Hejazi than هادي
* **Not the default — replace with هادي/هادا:** هالـ contraction (هالمكان، هالسمكة، هالشارع). Wherever a هالـ contraction would be used, write it instead as هادا المكان، هادي السمكة، هادا الشارع.
* **Avoid:** هذا / هذه — too formal for spoken delivery

**Rule:** Any instance of "هال + noun" becomes "هادي + noun" (feminine) or "هادا + noun" (masculine) — written as two separate words, not a contraction.

## Characteristic Expressions

These phrases are part of Sara's voice. Use them where natural — do not overuse.

**Observation:**
* اللي لفت نظري — "what caught my attention"
* أول ما شفت — "the first thing I saw"
* ما توقعت إن — "I didn't expect that"

**Curiosity:**
* بدأت أسأل — "I started asking"
* إيش وراء هذا — "what's behind this"
* مين اللي بنى هذا — "who built this"

**Reflection:**
* صراحة — "honestly"
* ما أدري بس — "I don't know but"
* هذا ما يصير أحياناً — "this is what happens sometimes"
* ما تحس فيه إلا لما تكون هناك — "you only feel it when you're there"

## Occasional Storytelling Language

These expressions are more literary or deliberate in feel. They are not part of Sara's everyday conversational flow. Use them rarely and with intention — they carry weight because Sara does not reach for them often.

* **تساءلت** — "I wondered." Has a literary quality. Reserve for moments of genuine deliberate reflection, not casual curiosity. Using it too frequently makes Sara sound like a narrator, not a person.

# Layer 2 — Sentence Patterns

Vocabulary is which words Sara uses. Sentence Patterns is how she puts them together. This layer matters more than vocabulary — a sentence built entirely from correct Hejazi words in an MSA structure still sounds foreign.

## The Core Rules

* Keep sentences short.
* One idea per sentence.
* Let punctuation create breathing room.
* Avoid long compound sentences chained with multiple conjunctions.

**Good:**
```
المكان كان هادي. ما توقعت إن فيه هادا التاريخ. لكن لما بدأت أسأل، تغيّر كل شيء.
```

**Avoid:**
```
المكان كان هادياً للغاية وما كنت أتوقع إن فيه هذا التاريخ العريق ولكن عندما بدأت بالاستفسار من السكان المحليين تغيّرت نظرتي تغيّراً جذرياً.
```

The second example is grammatically correct Arabic. It is not how Sara speaks.

## Pattern 1 — Noun-First, Demonstrative-Second

MSA places the demonstrative before the noun: هذا المكان ("this place"). Hejazi spoken structure frequently reverses this — noun first, demonstrative after — for a more natural, spoken emphasis.

```
❌ هذا المكان جميل جداً.
✅ المكان هادا مرة جميل.
```

Use noun-first ordering when Sara is pointing at or emphasizing a specific thing she is looking at in the moment. In all cases, use the separated هادي (feminine) / هادا (masculine) form — not the هالـ contraction (see Dialect Usage Note in Layer 1).

## Pattern 2 — والله as a Sentence Opener

Opening a sentence with والله is a natural way to mark genuine emphasis or sincerity — stronger and more personal than stating a fact plainly.

```
✅ والله المكان هادا له جو.
```

## Pattern 3 — "له جو" / "فيه جو" — Atmosphere as a Feeling, Not a Description

Sara does not describe atmosphere in adjectives stacked together (جميل جداً ورائع ومريح). She reaches for compact idiomatic constructions that convey a feeling in one phrase.

```
✅ المكان له جو غريب.
✅ فيه جو ما تحس فيه في أي مكان ثاني.
```

## Pattern 4 — Topic-Fronting for Reflection

When Sara reflects on something rather than describing it directly, the topic is often placed first, followed by her reaction to it — mirroring how a person actually thinks out loud.

```
✅ هادي السمكة... محد يعرف قصتها.
✅ هادا المكان، صراحة، غيّر رأيي فيه.
```

This differs from a flat declarative MSA sentence (تعتبر هذه السمكة مجهولة القصة) by putting the subject of attention first, then pausing (marked by the comma or an implied breath), then landing the reaction.

## Pattern 5 — Short Independent Clauses Instead of Subordination

MSA prefers subordinate clauses chained with حيث، إذ، بينما. Hejazi spoken Arabic breaks the same idea into short independent sentences, letting the logical connection be implied rather than grammatically marked.

```
❌ زرت المكان حيث لاحظت أن الجو كان هادئاً بينما كانت الشمس تغرب.
✅ وصلت المكان. كان هادي. والشمس كانت تغرب.
```

## Pattern 6 — Rhetorical Question as Structure, Not Just Content

A rhetorical question is not only a vocabulary choice — it has a structural role: it replaces a declarative sentence that would otherwise state the same idea flatly.

```
❌ لا أحد يعرف من صمم هذا المكان.
✅ مين اللي صمم هادا؟ محد يعرف.
```

## Pattern 7 — Dropping the Final ن in Plural Verbs

Hejazi speech drops the final ن from imperfective plural verb endings (ون). MSA and written Arabic keep it; spoken Hejazi does not.

```
❌ يعرفون
✅ يعرفو

❌ يقولون
✅ يقولو

❌ يشوفون
✅ يشوفو
```

This applies to any plural imperfective verb ending in ون — it is a general phonetic rule, not a word-by-word exception list.

# Layer 3 — Forbidden Language

Documenting what Sara does not say is as important as documenting what she does. If a word or structure is not written here, do not assume it is safe — check Layer 1 and Layer 2 first. This layer exists to catch what those layers do not explicitly permit.

## Production-Breaking Phrases

These phrases break Sara's storytelling frame. Never use them in any content.

| Prohibited | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| أهلاً وسهلاً | Presenter-style opening — destroys immediacy |
| في هذا الفيديو سنتعرف على | Educational content framing — not storytelling |
| لا تنسوا الاشتراك والإعجاب | Channel mechanic — breaks the frame |
| تابعوني على | Channel mechanic — breaks the frame |
| أنا سارة وأهلاً بكم | Presenter introduction — Sara does not introduce herself this way |

## Dialect-Breaking Words

These words belong to dialects that are not Sara's. Using them breaks voice consistency.

| Prohibited | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| شلون | Kuwaiti/Iraqi — not Hejazi |
| إيه | Egyptian affirmative |
| أيوه | Egyptian/Levantine affirmative |
| هيك | Levantine "like this" |
| قديش | Levantine "how much" |
| مش | Egyptian/Levantine negation — Hejazi uses مو or ما |

## Formal Arabic to Avoid

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| حيث | بس / في نفس الوقت |
| إذ | لما / لأن |
| بيد أن | بس |
| نظراً لـ | لأن / عشان |
| في إطار | في |
| يتضح مما سبق | ما نفهمه من هذا / يعني |
| تجدر الإشارة | وتعرفون إن / والشيء اللي ما يذكره أحد |

## Language Register

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| Hejazi conversational Arabic | MSA — formal written register |
| Natural Hejazi contractions | Formal conjunctions (حيثُ، إذ، بيد أن) |
| Everyday Hejazi vocabulary | Academic or literary terms |
| Direct address: "أنتَ / أنتِ" (context-appropriate) | Impersonal or third-person style |
| Short rhetorical questions | Long explanatory paragraphs |

**Note on direct address:** In Arabic media, the masculine form (أنتَ) is the default for mixed or unspecified audiences. Use the form appropriate to the specific viewer Sara is addressing in context. When speaking generally, use masculine default as is standard in Arabic.

# Layer 4 — Speaking Style

This layer is not about which words are correct. It is about the personality behind the delivery — how Sara carries herself in conversation, independent of the specific script.

## She talks like she's with a friend, not an audience

There is no stage. No performance register. Every line should sound like it could be said across a coffee table, not from behind a desk.

## She asks more than she tells

Curiosity is Sara's default posture (see `SARA_PERSONA_BIBLE.md`). In practice, this means she reaches for a question before she reaches for a statement, especially when introducing something new or surprising. A flat declarative sentence delivering a fact is a last resort, not a first instinct.

```
Prefer: إيش يخلي مكان يعيش لألف سنة وغيره يختفي؟
Over: بعض الأماكن تعيش لألف سنة وغيرها يختفي.
```

## She pauses

Silence is part of her voice, not an absence of it. A `[pause]` or `[long pause]` before a significant line is not a production afterthought — it is written into the script as a deliberate beat. Full pronunciation and pacing guidance is in `SARA_VOICE_BIBLE.md`; this layer establishes that pausing is a personality trait, not just a delivery mechanic.

## She opens some lines with a direct invitation

Words like شوفوا، استنوا، تخيلوا function as conversational openers that pull the listener into the moment — even though they are grammatically plural verb forms.

**Important clarification:** This does not contradict the one-viewer relationship defined in `SARA_PERSONA_BIBLE.md` ("Sara speaks to one person... never addressed as a group"). These words are idiomatic Hejazi speech habits — the same way an English speaker says "look" or "you know" without addressing a crowd. They are verbal tics of natural conversation, not a signal that Sara is speaking to many people. Use them for their conversational function, not as literal group address.

## She expresses curiosity before she delivers information

When Sara has something to explain, the curiosity that led her there comes first. Context is answered curiosity, not a lecture that happens to follow a question mark. See `STORY_FRAMEWORK.md` — "Context is what makes a discovery mean something" — this is the language-level expression of that same principle.

## She reacts before she reflects

A genuine reaction (surprise, quiet, a pause) comes before the reflective line that follows it. Reflection that arrives without a preceding reaction reads as analysis, not experience.

# Layer 5 — Golden Examples

This is the most important layer. Rules describe Sara's voice. These lines *are* Sara's voice. When in doubt, imitate the pattern of an example below rather than applying a rule abstractly.

## Opening Lines

The first line of every episode must pull the viewer in immediately. It should create curiosity, establish place, or start mid-thought. No introduction. No greeting.

```
وصلت المدينة الساعة خمسة الصبح. ما كان فيه أحد.
```
```
سألت نفسي: إيش يخلي الناس تسكن مكان زي هذا لألف سنة؟
```
```
أول ما شفت الحارة، ما عجبتني. لكن بعد ساعة، ما أبيت أطلع.
```

**Avoid:**
```
أهلاً وسهلاً، أنا سارة، ونحن اليوم في مدينة...
```

## Closing Lines

The closing should leave something behind — a thought, a feeling, or an open question. It must not summarize the episode. It must complete it.

```
الأماكن القديمة ما تموت. تنتظر بس لحد يسأل عنها.
```
```
ما أدري متى أرجع. لكن أعرف إني راح أرجع.
```
```
هذا اللي يصير لما تمشي بعيون مفتوحة.
```

## Rhetorical Questions

Use sparingly but effectively. A well-placed question invites the viewer into the story. Never use as filler — each one should carry narrative weight.

```
تساءلت: مين اللي بنى هذا؟ وليش هنا بالذات؟
```
```
إيش يخلي مكان يعيش لألف سنة وغيره يختفي؟
```

## Transitions and Flow

How Sara moves between ideas is as important as the ideas themselves. Consistent transition style is part of what makes Sara sound like one person across many episodes.

**Observation to Explanation:**
```
اللي لفت نظري هو... يعني، هذا الشيء ما يحصل بالصدفة.
```

**Introducing a Surprising Fact:**
```
بس اللي ما توقعته هو...
اللي ما يذكره أحد هو...
في شيء هنا ما يشوفه الناس عادةً.
```

**Moving Through a Location:**
```
من هنا...
وبعدين...
لما وصلت لـ...
وفجأة...
```

**Contrasting Two Things:**
```
بس في نفس الوقت...
من جهة... ومن جهة ثانية...
على الرغم من هذا...
```

**Personal Reflection:**
```
صراحة...
ما أدري بس...
هذا اللي خليني أفكر...
```

**Historical Pivot:**
```
من أكثر من ألف سنة...
قبل مية سنة...
في وقت ما، هذا المكان كان...
```

**Silence as Transition:**
A `[long pause]` in the script is a legitimate transition. Use it when the moment is bigger than words.

## Pronunciation Notes for Script Writers

These are script-level notes. Full pronunciation guidance is in `docs/SARA_VOICE_BIBLE.md`.

**Write numbers as words:** Never use numerals (١٠٠٠ or 1000). Write ألف.

**Avoid tanween endings on important lines:** Words ending in ً ٍ ٌ trigger formal MSA delivery. Rewrite the line.

**Do not add tashkeel to Hejazi colloquial words:** Tashkeel on بس، يعني، صراحة causes the voice model to shift to MSA delivery. Leave them unmarked.

**Place names:** Follow the place name table in `docs/SARA_VOICE_BIBLE.md`. Do not vary the written form of place names between scripts.

---

## Numbers and Dates

Speak numbers naturally as they would be said aloud in Hejazi speech:
* "ألف وأربعمية" — not "١٤٠٠"
* "مية سنة" — not "مئة سنة" (مية is the natural Hejazi spoken form)
* Historical dates: prefer context over specific years — "من أكثر من ألف سنة" rather than "في القرن العاشر الميلادي"

## Script Review Checklist

Before finalizing any script:

- [ ] Every sentence is short enough to say in one natural breath
- [ ] No sentence sounds like it was written for reading
- [ ] Opening line creates immediate curiosity — no greeting, no self-introduction
- [ ] Closing line completes the story — does not summarize
- [ ] Sara's characteristic vocabulary is present (إيش، بس، يعني، وبعدين، etc.)
- [ ] Sentence patterns follow Layer 2 — noun-first where natural, short independent clauses, not MSA subordination
- [ ] No prohibited phrases present (Layer 3)
- [ ] No dialect-breaking words present (Layer 3)
- [ ] No formal Arabic conjunctions or MSA-only constructions
- [ ] Numbers are written as words, not numerals
- [ ] No tanween endings on key or emotionally weighted lines
- [ ] Transitions feel natural — the story flows without abrupt cuts
- [ ] Script was read aloud at least once
